Meyer was awarded the Abel Prize in 2017.\n\nContents\n\n1 Biography\n2 Publications\n3 Awards and recognitions\n4 See also\n5 References\n6 External links\n\n\nBiography[edit]\nBorn in Paris to a Jewish family,[1] Yves Meyer studied at the Lyc\u00e9e Carnot in Tunis;[2] he won the French General Student Competition (Concours G\u00e9n\u00e9ral) in Mathematics, and was placed first in the entrance examination for the \u00c9cole Normale Sup\u00e9rieure in 1957.[3] He obtained his Ph.D. in 1966, under the supervision of Jean-Pierre Kahane.[4][5]  His cousin is the Mexican historian Jean Meyer.\nHe was teacher at the Prytan\u00e9e national militaire (1960\u20131963), a teaching assistant at the Universit\u00e9 de Strasbourg (1963\u20131966), a Professor at Universit\u00e9 Paris-Sud (1966\u20131980), a Professor at \u00c9cole Polytechnique (1980\u20131986), a Professor at Universit\u00e9 Paris-Dauphine (1985\u20131995), a Senior Researcher at the Centre national de la recherche and has been a Professor Emeritus at Ecole Normale Sup\u00e9rieure de Cachan since 2004.\nHe was awarded the 2010 Gauss Prize for fundamental contributions to number theory, operator theory and harmonic analysis, and his pivotal role in the development of wavelets and multiresolution analysis.[4] He also received the 2017\u00a0Abel Prize \"for his pivotal role in the development of the
