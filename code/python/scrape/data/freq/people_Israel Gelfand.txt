According to his own account, Gelfand was expelled from high school under the Soviets because his father had been a mill owner. Bypassing both high school and college, he proceeded to postgraduate study at the age of 19 at Moscow State University, where his advisor was the preeminent mathematician Andrei Kolmogorov.[2]\n\nWork[edit]\nGelfand is known for many developments including:\n\nthe book Calculus of Variations (1963), which he co-authored with Sergei Fomin\nthe Gelfand representation in Banach algebra theory;\nthe Gelfand\u2013Mazur theorem in Banach algebra theory;\nthe Gelfand\u2013Naimark theorem;\nthe Gelfand\u2013Naimark\u2013Segal construction;\nGelfand\u2013Shilov spaces\nthe Gelfand\u2013Pettis integral;\nthe representation theory of the complex classical Lie groups;\ncontributions to the theory of Verma modules in the representation theory of semisimple Lie algebras (with I. N. Bernstein and S. I. Gelfand);\ncontributions to distribution theory and measures on infinite-dimensional spaces;[3]\nthe first observation of the connection of automorphic forms with representations (with Sergei Fomin);\nconjectures about the Atiyah\u2013Singer index theorem;\nOrdinary differential equations (Gelfand\u2013Levitan theory);\nwork on calculus of variations and soliton theory (Gelfand\u2013Dikii equations);\ncontributions to the philosophy of cusp forms;\nGelfand\u2013Fuks cohomology of foliations;\nGelfand\u2013Kirillov dimension;\nintegral geometry;\ncombinatorial definition of the Pontryagin class;\nCoxeter functors;\ngeneral hypergeometric functions;\nGelfand\u2013Tsetlin patterns;\nGelfand-Lokutsievski method\nand many other results, particularly in the representation theory for the classical groups.\nI.M. Gelfand's seminar at Moscow State University\nwas running from 1945(?) until May 1989 (then it continued at Rutgers University), covered a wide range of topics,\nand was an important school for many mathematicians.[4][5][6]\n\nInfluence outside mathematics[edit]\nThe Gelfand\u2013Tsetlin (also spelled Zetlin) basis is a widely used tool in theoretical physics and the result of Gelfand's work on the representation theory of the unitary group and Lie groups in general.\nGelfand also published works on biology and medicine.[7] For a long time he took an interest in cell biology and organized a research seminar on the subject.[8][9]\nHe worked extensively in mathematics education, particularly with correspondence education. In 1994, he was awarded a MacArthur Fellowship for this work.\n\nFamily[edit]\nGelfand was married to Zorya Shapiro\u00a0[ru], and their two sons, Sergei and Vladimir both live in the United States.  The third son, Aleksandr, died of leukemia.  Following the divorce from his first wife, Gelfand married his second wife, Tatiana; together they had a daughter, Tatiana. The family also includes four grandchildren and three great-grandchildren.[10][11]\nMemories about I. Gelfand are collected at a dedicated website handled by his family.[12]\n\nHonors and awards[edit]\nGelfand held several honorary degrees and was awarded the Order of Lenin three times for his research.  In 1977 he was elected a Foreign Member of the Royal Society. He won the Wolf Prize in 1978, Kyoto Prize in 1989 and MacArthur Foundation Fellowship in 1994. He held the presidency of the Moscow Mathematical Society between 1968 and 1970, and was elected a foreign member of the U.S. National Academy of Science, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, the Royal Irish Academy, the American Mathematical Society and the London Mathematical Society.\nIn an October 2003 article in The New York Times, written on the occasion of his 90th birthday, Gelfand is described as a scholar who is considered \"among the greatest mathematicians of the 20th century\",[13] having exerted a tremendous influence on the field both through his own works and those of his students.\n\nDeath[edit]\nIsrael Gelfand died at the Robert Wood Johnson University Hospital near his home in Highland Park, New Jersey.  He was less than five weeks past his 96th birthday.  His death was first reported on the blog of his former collaborator Andrei Zelevinsky[14] and confirmed a few hours later by an obituary in the Russian online newspaper Polit.ru.[15]\n\nPublications[edit]\nGelfand, I.
