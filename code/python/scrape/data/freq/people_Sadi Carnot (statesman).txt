His third given name Sadi was in honour of his uncle Nicolas L\u00e9onard Sadi Carnot, the engineer who formulated the second law of thermodynamics and is generally regarded as the founder of the subject, named after the famed Persian poet Sadi of Shiraz. Like his uncle, Marie Fran\u00e7ois too came to be known as Sadi Carnot. In his scientific-mindedness and Republican leanings, he resembled his grandfather, Lazare Carnot, the military modernizer and member of the post-Revolutionary French Directory. Marie Fran\u00e7ois was educated as a civil engineer and was a highly distinguished student at both the \u00c9cole Polytechnique and the \u00c9cole des Ponts et Chauss\u00e9es. After his academic course, he obtained an appointment in the public service. His hereditary republicanism caused the government of national defence to entrust him in 1870 with the task of organizing resistance in the d\u00e9partements of the Eure, Calvados and Seine-Inf\u00e9rieure, and he was made prefect of Seine-Inf\u00e9rieure in January 1871. In August 1878 he was appointed secretary to the minister of public works. He assumed office at a critical period, when the republic was all but openly attacked by General Boulanger.[2]\n President Carnot's ostensible part during this agitation was confined to augmenting his popularity by well-timed appearances on public occasions, which gained credit for the presidency and the republic.
