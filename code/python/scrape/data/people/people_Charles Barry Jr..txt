{"name": "Charles Barry Jr.", "content": "Charles Barry Jr. (1823\u20131900) was an English architect of the mid-late 19th century, and eldest son of Sir Charles Barry. Like his younger brother and fellow architect Edward Middleton Barry, Charles Jr. designed numerous buildings in London. He is particularly associated with works in the south London suburb of Dulwich.\n Charles Jr. worked extensively on projects in London and East Anglia with fellow architect Robert Richardson Banks (1812\u201372), working from an office in Sackville Street,[1] and then collaborated with his shorter-lived brother Edward on several schemes.\n Charles Sr. had been architect and surveyor to Dulwich College, designing the Grammar School, among other buildings. Charles Jr. then succeeded his father in the role. He designed the New College (1866\u201370) \u2013 a building of red brick and white stone, designed in a hybrid of Palladian and Gothic styles.\n His other projects include:\n Charles Jr. was elected a Fellow of the Society of Antiquaries of London in 1876, and was a member of the Society's Council in 1878. He was President of the Royal Institute of British Architects from 1876-79. He was also awarded the prestigious RIBA Royal Gold Medal in 1877. His pupils included Sir Aston Webb (himself a later President of the RIBA and winner of the Royal Gold Medal).\n He lived in a large villa \"Lapsewood\" in Sydenham Hill. His son was Lt Col Arthur John Barry CBE, TD, MICE (b. 21 November 1859), civil engineer and architect. A. J.
