From the style of his writing, it is known that he lived not much later than Archimedes.\nHe is mentioned in Diocles' On Burning Mirrors:\n\nAnd when Zenodorus the astronomer came down to Arcadia and was introduced to us, he asked us how to find a mirror surface such that when it is placed facing the sun the rays reflected from it meet a point and thus cause burning.[1]\nZenodorus is known for authoring the treatise On isoperimetric figures, now lost. Many of its propositions are known from Theon of Alexandria's commentary on Ptolemy's Syntaxis.  In his On isoperimetric figures, Zenodorus studies the areas and perimeters of different geometric figures.  The most important propositions proved by him are that,\n\nOf all regular polygons of equal perimeter, that is the greatest in area which has the most angles.\nA circle is greater than any regular polygon of equal contour.\n Of all polygons of the same number of sides and equal perimeter the equilateral and equiangular polygon is the greatest in area.\nOf all solid figures the surfaces of which are equal, the sphere is the greatest in solid content..[2][3]\nNotes[edit]\n\n^ Toomer (1976)\n\n^ Heath (1981) p. 207\u2013213\n\n^ Kline (1972), p. 126\n\n\nReferences[edit]\nHeath, Thomas Little (1981).
