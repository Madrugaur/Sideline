He had been a member of the Communist Party since 1944. He earned his doctorate in 1952. His wife Valentina was the daughter of Revolutionary hero Nikolay Shchors.\nMuch of Khalatnikov's research was a collaboration with, or inspired by, Lev Landau, including the Landau-Khalatnikov theory of superfluidity.\nDuring 1969 he briefly worked as a part time professor of theoretical physics at Leiden University.[1]\nIn 1970, inspired by the mixmaster model introduced by Charles W. Misner, then at Princeton University, Khalatnikov, together with Vladimir Belinski and Evgeny Lifshitz, introduced what has become known as the BKL conjecture, which is widely regarded as one of the most outstanding open problems in the classical theory of gravitation.\nKhalatnikov directed the Landau Institute for Theoretical Physics in Moscow from 1965 to 1992. He was elected to the Academy of Sciences of the Soviet Union in 1984. He has been awarded the Landau Gold Medal, the Humboldt Prize, and the Marcel Grossmann Award. He was also a foreign member of the Royal Society of London.\nHe was portrayed by actor Georg Nikoloff in the film The Theory of Everything.\nKhalatnikov died in Chernogolovka on 9 January 2021, aged 101.[2]\n\nHonours and awards[edit]\nOrder \"For Merit to the Fatherland\", 3rd class\nOrder of the October Revolution\nOrder of the Patriotic War, 2nd class\nOrder of the Red Banner of Labour, three times\nOrder of Friendship of Peoples\nOrder of the Badge of Honour\nStalin Prize (1953)\nMarcel Grossmann Award (2012) \"For the discovery of a general solution of the Einstein equations with a cosmological singularity of an oscillatory chaotic character known as the BKL singularity\"\nAsteroid 468725 Khalat was named in his honor.[3] The official naming citation was published by the Minor Planet Center on 18 May 2019 (M.P.C. 114955).[4]\nPartial bibliography[edit]\nBooks[edit]\nKhalatnikov, I.
