{"name": "Juliusz Schauder", "content": "Juliusz Pawe\u0142 Schauder ([\u02c8jul\u02b2ju\u0282\u00a0\u02c8pav\u025bw\u00a0\u02c8\u0282au\u032fd\u025br]; 21 September 1899, Lw\u00f3w, Austria-Hungary  \u2013 September 1943, Lw\u00f3w, Occupied Poland) was a Polish mathematician of Jewish origin, known for his work in functional analysis, partial differential equations and mathematical physics.\n Born on 21 September 1899 in Lw\u00f3w, he had to fight in World War I right after his graduation from school. He was captured and imprisoned in Italy. He entered the university in Lw\u00f3w in 1919 and received his doctorate in 1923. He got no appointment at the university and continued his research while working as teacher at a secondary school. Due to his outstanding results, he obtained a scholarship in 1932 that allowed him to spend several years in Leipzig and, especially, Paris. Even before the Lwow ghetto was established he wrote to Ludwig Bieberbach pleading for his support. Instead, Bieberbach passed his letter to the Gestapo and Schauder was arrested. In his letters to Swiss mathematicians, he wrote that he had important new results, but no paper to write them down.
