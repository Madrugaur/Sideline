{"name": "Jacques-Germain Soufflot", "content": "Jacques-Germain Soufflot (22 July 1713 \u2013 29 August 1780) was a French architect in the international circle that introduced neoclassicism. His most famous work is the Panth\u00e9on in Paris, built from 1755 onwards, originally as a church dedicated to Saint Genevieve.\n Soufflot was born in Irancy, near Auxerre. In the 1730s he attended the French Academy in Rome, where young French students in the 1750s would later produce the first full-blown generation of Neoclassical designers. Soufflot's models were less the picturesque Baroque being built in modern Rome, as much as the picturesque aspects of monuments of antiquity.\n After returning to France, Soufflot practiced in Lyon, where he built the H\u00f4tel-Dieu, like a chaste riverside street facade, interrupted by the central former chapel, its squared dome with illusionistic diminishing coffers on the interior. With the Temple du Change, he was entrusted with completely recasting a 16th-century market exchange building housing a meeting space housed above a loggia. Soufflot's newly made loggia is an unusually severe arcading tightly bound between flat Doric pilasters, with emphatic horizontal lines. He was accepted into the Lyon Academy.\n A more creative trip to Italy was made when the mature Soufflot returned in 1750 in the company of the future Marquis de Marigny, the talented young brother of Madame de Pompadour, who was being groomed for his future as director of the King's Buildings (B\u00e2timents du Roi). On this trip Soufflot made a special study of theaters. In 1755 Marigny, the new Director General of Royal Buildings, gave Soufflot architectural control of all the royal buildings in Paris. In the same year, he was admitted to the Royal Academy of Architecture. In 1756 his opera house opened in Lyon.\n The Panth\u00e9on is his most famous work, but the H\u00f4tel Marigny built for his young patron (1768\u20131771) across from the \u00c9lys\u00e9e Palace, is a better definition of Soufflot's personal taste. Soufflot died in Paris in 1780.\n Like all the architects of his day, Soufflot considered the classical idiom essential.
