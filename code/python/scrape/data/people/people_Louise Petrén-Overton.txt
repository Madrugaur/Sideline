had earned a doctorate in mathematics in 1850, and her great-uncle Carl Johan Hill\u00a0[sv] had been a professor of mathematics at Lund University.[2]\nOne of her brothers was physician and researcher Karl Anders Petr\u00e9n.[1]\nWith two older sisters doing the housework, she was left free to concentrate on her studies.[1][2]\nAs a child, ill with scarlet fever, she told her family that she would not go to heaven unless she could bring her mathematics books there.[2]\nShe earned an education certificate through private tutoring in 1899,\nand earned a bachelor's degree at Lund University in 1902,\nas one of roughly a dozen women at the university and the only one in the sciences.[2] She earned a licenciate in 1910 and defended her doctorate at Lund in 1911, with the dissertation Extension de la m\u00e9thode de Laplace aux \u00e9quations.[1]\n\nContributions[edit]\nNail Ibragimov writes that Petr\u00e9n \"made a profound contribution to the constructive integration theory of partial differential equations in the direction initiated by Euler and continued by Laplace, Legendre, Imschenetsky, Darboux, Goursat. In her PhD thesis she extended to higher-order equations Laplace\u2019s method of integration of second-order linear hyperbolic equations with two independent variables.\"[3]\n\nLater life[edit]\nPetr\u00e9n married Ernest Overton, a professor at Lund, but as a woman could not obtain a position there herself.
